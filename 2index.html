Scriptural Foundation

Why the Dark Moon Begins the Month

"And God said, Let there be lights in the firmament of the heaven to divide the day from the night; and let them be for signs, and for seasons, and for days, and years." - Genesis 1:14

The biblical month begins with the dark new moon, representing the start of the lunar cycle. Just as Yah created light from darkness, each month begins in darkness and progresses to light.

"Blow up the trumpet in the new moon, in the time appointed, on our solemn feast day." - Psalms 81:3

The Hebrew word for "new moon" (chodesh) comes from the root word meaning "to renew" or "to repair." This indicates the beginning of the lunar cycle when the moon is not visible, before it begins to show light again.

Sabbath: 7-Day Cycles After the New Moon

"Remember the sabbath day, to keep it holy. Six days shalt thou labour, and do all thy work: But the seventh day is the sabbath of the LORD thy God..." - Exodus 20:8-10

The weekly Sabbath follows a seven-day cycle that begins with the new moon. Each month resets this cycle, with Sabbaths falling on the 7th, 14th, 21st, and 28th days of the lunar month.

Sabbath Pattern in Scripture:

  • Creation pattern: Six days of work, seventh day of rest (Genesis 2:2-3)
  • Manna collection: Six days gathering, none on Sabbath (Exodus 16:23-26)
  • Fourth Commandment: Keep the seventh day holy (Exodus 20:8-11)

New Moon & Sabbath Connection:

  • New Moon and Sabbath mentioned together (Isaiah 66:23)
  • Different observances for New Moon vs. Sabbath (Ezekiel 46:1)
  • New Moon as a distinct appointed time (Numbers 10:10)

Feast Day Calculations with Seasonal Markers

"These are the feasts of the LORD, even holy convocations, which ye shall proclaim in their seasons." - Leviticus 23:4

Yah's appointed feast days are calculated based on the lunar calendar, with specific days of specific months designated for each observance. The first month (Aviv) is determined by the ripening of barley in Israel, aligning the calendar with agricultural seasons.
